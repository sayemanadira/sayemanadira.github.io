Simulating Canonical Ensemble using Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

Spring 2025 Computational Physics Partner(s): Richard Zheng Julia
Parallelization Leapfrog Integration

Overview

This project investigates the use of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) for simulating classical thermodynamic systems under the canonical ensemble. Traditional Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods like Metropolis-Hastings struggle in high-dimensional spaces, often failing to efficiently explore the narrow “typical set” where most probability mass lies. HMC addresses these limitations by leveraging Hamiltonian dynamics to propose informed transitions through phase space, enabling faster convergence and significantly higher acceptance rates, even in high dimensions.

Technical Details

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) is a smart way to explore complicated probability spaces. Instead of taking random steps like basic methods, HMC borrows ideas from physics to move more efficiently through space. Imagine each possible state of a system as a point with both a position and momentum. These are combined into an "energy" using a formula called the Hamiltonian. HMC uses this energy to simulate how a particle would move, similar to tracing a smooth path through a landscape. At each step, HMC: 1. Randomly picks a momentum (like giving the particle a push). 2. Simulates its motion using simple physics equations. 3. Proposes the new position as the next sample. This method creates long, informed steps that help cover space faster and more accurately than traditional random-walk methods—especially helpful in high-dimensional problems. The simulation was implemented in Julia and parallelized using Threads.@threads to accelerate Leapfrog integration across particles.

Results

The system was validated against theoretical models: for an ideal gas, HMC accurately reproduced the classical result Ē = (3/2)NkT; when adding a harmonic potential, the average energy doubled as predicted by the equipartition theorem. The method was also tested on generalized Hamiltonians of the form E = A|→p|s, revealing how increasing the exponent s or spatial dimension d impacts average energy. These results confirm the effectiveness of HMC in capturing thermodynamic behavior and demonstrate its scalability for simulating high-dimensional and non-trivial systems.
